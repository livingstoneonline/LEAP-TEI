Linyanti 8th November
1853


Revd Dr Tidman


Dear Sir


5

By a letter dated September last
I informed you of my intention to proceed Westward in
order to open up a way to the coast. I was then waiting
for rains and as these have now commenced I leave
this on the 10th currt. I have altered my mind with
10respect to the route which I intend to try. We sent
men to the Westward in order to examine if there is any
strip of country free of tsetse in that direction - their
report was unfavourable. I then resolved to go on
the trail of the Mambari but the slave merchant
15signalized his departure by seizing two men and
a woman as slaves. The Makololo assembeled im-
mediately and compelled him to unchain the captives
This affair made me alter my resolution ^ again for if I
followed his footsteps the different tribes through which
20we have to pass would naturally believe me to be
of the same clan. Indeed knowing what my
precursors had done, I should not feel very respectable
to myself. I therefore intend going up the river
Leeambye to the point at which we turned in
25August and then ascend the Leeba to the people
called Balonda and proceed westward on foot
by land. The chief has lent me his own canoe 0002
and as I was unable to take a horse to the Barotse
country on account of the tsetse at this season biting by
night as well as by day Sekeletu placed four riding oxen
at my service, These are now in that country so I hope
5to get on. I go in the canoe from this down the Chobe
to the confluence. Then up the Leeambye and Leeba till
we reach the falls reported to exist on that river. The canoe
will be sent back and we must do the best we can
beyond. I leave in company with Barotse people
10alone. The people we brought from Kuruman have
been rendered useless by fever, so I send them
back. I am again through God's mercy and kindness
quite recovered from the effects of that disease. I
think I am getting rid of intermittent too and
15if spared will impart some knowledge of Christ
to many who never before heard his blessed name
There are many and very large tribes in the direction
in which we go. All are sitting in darkness and
the shadow of death. I hope God will in mercy
20permit me to establish the gospel somewhere
in this region, and that I may live to see
the double influence of the spirit of commerce
and Christianity employed to stay the bitter fountain
of African misery

My letter of September contained three enclosures
for Mrs Livingston. It contained full information
respecting the visit to the Barotse, this opportunity
of transmitting letters was unexpected but though I had
